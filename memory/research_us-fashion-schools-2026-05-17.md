# US Fashion-Business Education: Operational Buying / Merchandising / Planning Curriculum

> Research date: 2026-05-17
> Purpose: ground aimily In-Season's decision verbs and vocabulary in what is **actually taught** to working buyers and planners at the US programs that feed Macy's, Bloomingdale's, Saks, Nordstrom, J.Crew, Gap, Anthropologie. The US programs (LIM + FIT especially) are intentionally vocational — they teach the spreadsheet, the meeting ritual, and the decision rules. That is exactly what aimily In-Season needs as ground truth.

---

## 0. Executive summary — 10 hard bullets

1. **FIT's BS in Fashion Business Management is the most operationally complete buying-planning curriculum in the US.** The core "planning stack" is FM 224 (Merchandising Math Applications) → FM 322 (Merchandise Product Planning & Analytics) → FM 325 (Financial Assortment and Planning, taught on JDA Enterprise Planning software, builds an actual six-month plan) → FM 423 (Fashion Planning and Allocation, multi-door retailer case-study lab) → FM 422 (Merchandising Strategies, capstone). FM 329 (Predictive Analytics for Planning and Forecasting) is the closest analogue to aimily In-Season's mission.
2. **The canonical operational textbook on retail math is Tepper & Greene, *Mathematics for Retail Buying* (8th ed., Bloomsbury / Fairchild Books, ISBN 978-1-5013-1565-7).** Chapter 5 ("Dollar Planning and Control" — Six-Month Plan and Open-to-Buy Control) is the single most-assigned chapter in US merchandising programs. The current edition includes a new "Formulating a Six-Month Plan" case study and an expanded assortment-planning section.
3. **The second canonical text is Donnellan, *Merchandise Buying and Management* (4th ed., Fairchild Books / Bloomsbury, ISBN 978-1-6090-1490-2).** Operative chapters: Ch 12 "Retail pricing" (markup, markdowns, types of markdowns, clearance, slow sellers, maintained markup, tactical price changes, managing markdowns) and Ch 13 "Planning sales and inventory" (open-to-buy). This is the assigned text in FIT FM 224 and in LIM FASH 2560.
4. **Levy, Weitz & Grewal, *Retailing Management* (11th ed., McGraw-Hill, ISBN 978-1-2641-5744-0)** is the strategy-level text used in NYU Stern Luxury & Retail MBA and at undergraduate Section III "Merchandise Management" — Ch 12 "Managing the Merchandise Planning Process", Ch 13 "Buying Merchandise", Ch 14 "Retail Pricing". Less operational than Donnellan/Tepper, more strategic. Barton A. Weitz is the long-standing co-author who anchors the academic side.
5. **The Monday Morning trade meeting** is taught explicitly in FIT FM 423 and LIM's Retail Buying & Planning concentration as a four-section ritual: (a) **Sales** YTD/MTD/LW vs plan; (b) **Product performance** best/worst by $, units, margin, sell-through %; (c) **Stock alignment** stock vs sales variance, weeks of cover, size availability; (d) **Intake visibility** OTB, replenishment, clearance. The buyer presents, the planner challenges, the DMM signs off action.
6. **The canonical decision verbs taught in US programs** are: **Reorder / Replenish**, **Mark down** (initial, second, terminal/clearance), **Cancel / Reduce on Order**, **Transfer** (between doors), **Promote / Push** (signage, placement, marketing), **Maintain** (no action), and **Investigate** (variance > threshold but cause unclear). Note: there is no canonical classroom verb for "amplify winner" — US programs call this "**Reorder + Distort**" (i.e. lean the next OTB toward proven winners) which directly maps to aimily's `amplify`.
7. **SKU classification is taught universally as ABC/ABCD by sales velocity and contribution**, with the apparel-specific overlay of "**Basic / Fashion / Seasonal / Test**". The ABC split is the textbook 20/80, 30/15, 50/5 rule (Group A = 20% of SKUs / 80% of revenue). The "hero / dog" vernacular is industry slang — taught informally — but the classroom labels are **best sellers / worst sellers / underperformers / overstocks**.
8. **The Open-to-Buy formula taught universally** is: `OTB (retail) = Planned EOM Stock + Planned Sales + Planned Markdowns − Planned BOM Stock − Merchandise on Order`. Taught with `Stock-to-Sales Ratio Method` as the primary inventory-planning approach: `BOM = Stock-to-Sales Ratio × Planned Sales`. The closing identity is `EOM = BOM + Purchases − Sales − Retail Reductions`.
9. **The canonical markdown ladder taught in US programs** is a three-step cadence: **Initial markdown −25 to −30% / hold 3 weeks → Second markdown −40 to −60% / hold 3 weeks → Terminal/clearance −70%+ to liquidation date**. The trigger is sell-through vs the **plan curve** ("sell-through by week of season" benchmark), not absolute sell-through. Tepper Ch 2 ("Retail Pricing and Repricing") teaches this; Donnellan Ch 12 reinforces with "managing markdowns" framework.
10. **Faculty-from-industry pipeline is strongest at LIM and FIT.** LIM's current president Elizabeth Marcuse came up through Macy's Executive Training Program → buying at Montgomery Ward, The Limited (Lane Bryant), Macy's → Director of Retail Planning at Donna Karan and Mamiye Sales. Adjunct Phyllis Shapiro has 30+ years buying/merchandising/sourcing. Marla Greene (LIM, also co-author of Tepper's 8th edition) was Merchandise Manager at Liz Claiborne Outlet Stores and Buyer at Bare Necessities, Belk Store Services, Kids R Us. FIT has Professor **Richard Jaffe** (Macy's Executive Training Program → petite sportswear buyer → retail analyst) and an associate professor (name not surfaced in public bios — referenced anonymously in FIT comms) who went **Bloomingdale's executive → Bergdorf Goodman buyer (launched "The Home on Seven") → MCM merchandising director → Macys.com launch lead 1997**.

### 1.3 NYU Stern — Fashion & Luxury MBA / Luxury & Retail MBA

NYU Stern launched the Fashion & Luxury MBA in 2017 — the first US business-school MBA focused on the sector. 52 credits in 1 year. Combines core MBA (finance, accounting, marketing, leadership) with industry-specific curriculum (retail strategy, supply chain, consumer behavior). Compulsory **Stern Solutions** consulting engagement with partner companies.

NYU Stern is **strategy-heavy, not operationally granular**. Students develop functional expertise in finance, marketing, merchandising, operations or new ventures, but there is no single course taught at the spreadsheet level the way FIT FM 325 is. Levy/Weitz/Grewal is the assigned text. NYU Stern faculty are academic (Weitz is at University of Florida historically; Levy at Babson; Grewal at Babson).

Imran Amed (BoF) appears in NYU Stern coverage on BoF but does not teach at NYU Stern. He has lectured/guest-spoken at Parsons and at Stern's "Meet NYU Stern" series.

### 1.4 Parsons School of Design (The New School) — BBA / MS Strategic Design and Management

Parsons' BBA SDM is **design-led-business**, not buying-led. First-year is the all-Parsons foundation (art and design concepts, skills). From year 2: design studios + seminars on branding, product/service development, innovation, law, finance, ethics, value alignment, sustainability.

There is **no buying course or retail-math course in the Parsons SDM curriculum**. The program is taught by industry specialists in design strategy, not by ex-buyers. Useful for brand-architecture thinking and consumer/positioning research, not for in-season operational decisions.

### 1.5 FIDM (Fashion Institute of Design & Merchandising, LA)

FIDM is more vocational than academic. **Merchandise Product Development** AA covers garment construction, market and trend research, textile identification, and **purchase order writing and calculation** (operational, not strategic). Classes capped ~30 students. Faculty come heavily from LA-market industry (Forever 21, Guess, BCBG, Hot Topic).

FIDM **does not surface** a six-month-plan or open-to-buy course as visibly as FIT does in its public catalog — buying mechanics are embedded in studio courses rather than abstracted into a math course.

## 2. The OPERATIONAL CANON distilled from US programs

This is the section that maps directly onto aimily In-Season's decision engine.

### 2.1 The Six-Month Plan (taught in FIT FM 325, LIM FASH 2560 / 3515, Marist, Drexel DSMR 232, Cal Poly Pomona, SCAD FASM 220)

Canonical structure as taught in classroom (Tepper Ch 5 / Donnellan Ch 13 / Levy-Weitz Ch 12):

```
                      Spring (Feb–Jul) or Fall (Aug–Jan)
                      Feb | Mar | Apr | May | Jun | Jul | TOTAL
Planned Sales         …
BOM Stock             … = Stock-to-Sales Ratio × Planned Sales
EOM Stock             … = next month's BOM
Stock-to-Sales Ratio  …
Markdowns             …
Retail Reductions     … = Markdowns + Shrinkage + Employee Discounts
Purchases (retail)    … = Sales + Markdowns + EOM − BOM
Purchases (cost)      … = Purchases retail × (1 − Markup %)
Markup %              …
Open-to-Buy           … = Purchases retail − Merchandise on Order
```

Decision orientation (taught explicitly):
1. **Top-down**: financial target (Sales $) drives Purchases $ which drives Open-to-Buy.
2. **Bottom-up**: assortment plan by category/class/SKU rolls up; reconciled against top-down.
3. **In-season variance**: when actual sales beat plan, OTB opens up (good — but watch stock-to-sales); when sales miss plan, EOM stock balloons, markdowns rise, OTB tightens or closes.

### 2.2 Open-to-Buy mechanics (the buyer's daily/weekly tool)

The canonical OTB formula taught universally (Tepper Ch 5; Donnellan Ch 13; Levy-Weitz Ch 12):

```
OTB (retail $) = Planned EOM Stock
               + Planned Sales (rest of month)
               + Planned Markdowns (rest of month)
               − Current BOM Stock (actual)
               − Merchandise on Order (committed but not yet received)
```

**Thresholds taught:**
- `OTB > 0`: buyer has dollars to spend. Verb taught: **Buy / Reorder / Place new order**.
- `OTB = 0`: balanced. Verb: **Hold / Maintain**.
- `OTB < 0` ("**over-bought**"): the buyer has committed beyond plan. Verbs: **Cancel** (reduce on-order), **Postpone** (push delivery to next month), **Mark down** (accelerate sell-through to restore plan), **Transfer** (between doors).

### 2.3 Sell-Through tracking (the weekly Monday ritual)

`Sell-through % = Units sold ÷ Units received (or original BOM stock) × 100`

The canonical tracking discipline taught in FIT FM 423 and LIM FASH 3515:

| Week of season | Sell-through plan curve (women's apparel, typical) | Action if actual < plan by 5+ pts | Action if actual > plan by 5+ pts |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1–2 (set-up) | 5–10% | Investigate: pricing, placement, allocation | Watch — could be promo or weather |
| 3–6 (full price) | 25–40% | Mark down trigger consideration | **Reorder + distort** future OTB |
| 7–10 (transition) | 45–65% | First markdown −25 to −30% | Lean future buys toward this style/color |
| 11–14 (clearance setup) | 65–80% | Second markdown −40 to −60% | Already winning — maintain |
| 15+ (clearance / terminal) | 80–95% | Terminal markdown −70%+; clearance | Pull early into next season hero list |

Cadence: meeting **every Monday morning**. Attendees: **Buyer** (presents the merchandise data and proposes action), **Planner** (challenges the financial impact, brings OTB context), **DMM/GMM** (signs off on markdown depth, reorder commits, cancel decisions), often **Allocator** (door-level, reports on stock-out and overstock by location).

### 2.4 The Markdown cadence (taught in Tepper Ch 2, Donnellan Ch 12)

Three-step ladder:

| Step | Depth from regular | Hold period | Trigger |
|---|---|---|---|
| Initial markdown | −25 to −30% | 3 weeks | Sell-through under plan curve by week N or weeks-of-cover > remaining-weeks-of-season |
| Second markdown | −40 to −60% | 3 weeks | Initial markdown failed to clear sufficient units; usually ~6 weeks before season end |
| Terminal / clearance | −70%+ to liquidation | Until sell-out or season-end | Final clearance to clear EOM stock before next season's set |

Taught caveat (Donnellan Ch 12 "Managing Markdowns"): the worst markdown is the one taken too late. The buyer's instinct is to wait and hope; the planner's job is to enforce the cadence. aimily In-Season's `markdown` verb maps cleanly to this — but the curriculum teaches that **markdown timing is more important than markdown depth**.

### 2.5 The SKU classification taxonomy (the vocabulary buyers learn in school)

**Velocity ABC (Lightspeed / Softeon / classroom standard):**
- Group A: top 20% of SKUs / 80% of revenue → tight control, frequent monitoring, low safety stock
- Group B: 30% of SKUs / 15% of revenue → moderate
- Group C: 50% of SKUs / 5% of revenue → relaxed controls, larger safety stock
- (Some programs add D — the long tail / candidates for deletion)

**Apparel overlay (Donnellan; FIT FM 322):**
- **Basics / Replenishment**: core items, year-round, replenished against model stock (e.g. white T-shirt). Decision verb: **Replenish to model**.
- **Fashion**: seasonal, time-limited, no replenishment after initial buy. Decision verb: **Markdown to clear** at season end.
- **Seasonal basics**: basics with seasonal flavor (e.g. swimwear, outerwear). Decision verb: hybrid.
- **Test / Trial**: small initial buys to read market. Decision verb: **Distort or kill** at week 4–6 read.

**The "hero / dog" vernacular** is industry slang — used in trade press, not in classroom textbooks. Classroom uses **best sellers / worst sellers / underperformers / overstocks**.

### 2.6 Key KPI vocabulary (canonical US merchandising)

| US classroom term | Formula | What it answers |
|---|---|---|
| Sell-through % | Units sold / Units received × 100 | How fast is this moving? |
| Weeks of cover (WOC) / Weeks of supply (WOS) | On-hand units / avg weekly sales | When do I run out? |
| Stock-to-sales ratio | BOM stock $ / Sales $ for the month | Am I overstocked? |
| Gross margin % | (Sales − COGS) / Sales × 100 | Margin health |
| Maintained markup % | Final margin after markdowns | True margin retained |
| **GMROI / GMROII** | Gross margin $ / Average inventory cost $ | Inventory profitability — target 3.0–3.5 for apparel |
| Open-to-buy $ | EOM + Sales + Markdowns − BOM − On Order | Dollars left to spend |
| Inventory turnover | Sales / Average inventory | How many times the inventory cycles |
| Markdown % | Markdowns $ / Sales $ × 100 | Markdown intensity |
| Initial markup (IMU) % | (Retail − Cost) / Retail × 100 | Price-setting margin |
| Sell-thru by week N | Cumulative sell-through at week N of season | Curve vs plan |
| Stock-out rate | % SKUs with zero on-hand | Lost-sales risk |

### 2.7 The canonical decision verbs in US merchandising classrooms

Ordered by frequency of use in Donnellan / Tepper / Levy-Weitz / FIT FM 423 case studies:

| Classroom verb | Trigger / context | aimily verb mapping |
|---|---|---|
| **Reorder / Replenish** | Sell-through > plan curve + WOC < lead time + season weeks remaining | `Reponer N uds` ✅ |
| **Reorder + Distort** | Style winning hard; commit incremental future OTB toward this lineage | `Replicar estilo` / `Extender colores` ✅ partial |
| **Mark down (initial)** | Sell-through < plan curve by 5+ pts at week 3–6 | `Markdown` ✅ |
| **Mark down (second / terminal)** | Initial markdown failed; ≤6 weeks to season end | `Markdown` (with depth) ✅ |
| **Cancel / Reduce on Order** | Over-bought, OTB < 0, or class trending below plan | `Reducir compra` ✅ |
| **Transfer** | Stock-out one door + overstock another door (multi-door retailers only) | not yet in aimily verb set ⚠️ |
| **Promote / Push** | Sell-through soft but margin healthy; lever signage/placement/email before markdown | `Investigar` partial — aimily folds this into investigate, classroom keeps separate |
| **Maintain** | On plan; no action | `Mantener` ✅ |
| **Investigate** | Variance > threshold, root cause unclear (e.g. allocation error, vendor delay, sizing) | `Investigar` ✅ |
| **Kill / Discontinue** | Test SKU read at week 4–6 fails; class consistently bottom-decile across colors | `Matar` (Kill) ✅ |

**Mismatches between aimily In-Season verbs and US classroom canon:**
- aimily's **`Replicar estilo`** and **`Extender colores con swatches del moodboard`** are **forward-looking creative levers** that fold "Reorder + Distort" plus "feed into next season's brief." This is NOT a single verb in the classroom — classroom teaches Reorder + Distort as a current-season action and treats next-season carry-over as a separate **Spring/Fall Buying Plan** discussion. aimily's combined verb is therefore a **product innovation**, not a translation gap, but should be communicated as "amplify this season's winner + signal to next season's plan" so buyers don't read it as a single confused action.
- aimily has **no Transfer verb**. For multi-door retailers (Zara, Mango, Inditex) this is a major operational lever. Recommend adding `Transferir entre tiendas` for multi-door datasets. The US programs teach this in FM 423's allocation lab as a primary in-season verb.
- aimily's **`Investigar`** conflates two classroom verbs: **Investigate** (cause unknown) and **Promote/Push** (cause known — soft sell-through with good margin — and the answer is marketing lever, not markdown). Splitting these would mirror the US classroom and reduce false-investigate noise.
- aimily's **`Kill`** maps directly to **Discontinue** in classroom usage. Note: classroom rarely uses "kill" — it's industry slang. The clean term is **Discontinue** or **Exit**.

### 2.8 The KPI gap aimily In-Season should close

Comparing aimily's current six-confidence-dim scoring against US classroom KPIs:

| Classroom KPI | Currently in aimily In-Season? | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|
| Sell-through % vs week-of-season plan curve | partial (raw sell-through yes, curve overlay no) | Add plan curve overlay as the headline read — this is what buyers read first on Monday |
| Weeks of cover / WOC | not surfaced | Add WOC as a primary signal; threshold = lead time |
| Stock-to-sales ratio | not surfaced | Compute from inventory_facts + sales_windows |
| GMROI | partial (margin_pct_list yes, GMROI no) | Compute and surface — this is **the** single number senior buyers cite |

| Stock-out rate by SKU-week | not surfaced | Critical for amplify_winner — a "winner" with stock-outs is under-attributed |
| Markdown % of sales | partial | Surface explicitly; trigger for kill/markdown ladder progression |

## 4. What's actually IN the canonical textbooks (beyond the academic canon)

### 4.1 Tepper & Greene, *Mathematics for Retail Buying*, 8th ed. (Bloomsbury / Fairchild, 2023)

| Ch | Title | Operational content |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Merchandising for a Profit | P&L structure, the profit equation |
| 2 | **Retail Pricing and Repricing of Merchandise** | Initial markup, additional markup, markdown, markdown cancellation, employee discount, promotional pricing |
| 3 | Basic Markup Equations Used in Merchandising Decisions | IMU formulas, cumulative markup, blended markup |
| 4 | The Retail Method of Inventory | RIM accounting, shrinkage, book-to-physical |
| 5 | **Dollar Planning and Control** | **The Six-Month Plan + Open-to-Buy Control.** Expanded in 8th ed. New assortment-planning section. New case study: "Formulating a Six-Month Plan." |
| 6 | Analysis of the Basic Sales Elements | Sales analysis by classification, week-by-week, plan vs actual |

This is the **operational core**. Chapter 5 is the chapter that maps onto aimily In-Season.

### 4.2 Donnellan, *Merchandise Buying and Management*, 4th ed. (Bloomsbury / Fairchild)

Operative chapters for aimily In-Season:
- **Ch 12 Retail Pricing**: markup, types of markup, markdowns, types of markdowns, clearance merchandise, slow sellers, maintained markup, **tactical price changes**, **managing markdowns**, markdown allowances (from vendors).
- **Ch 13 Planning Sales and Inventory**: open-to-buy, stock-to-sales ratio method, BOM/EOM planning, weeks-of-supply method.

Less mathematical than Tepper. More qualitative-decision oriented. **The chapter on "managing markdowns" is the single best ~30 pages on the operational decision aimily In-Season is automating.**

### 4.3 Levy, Weitz & Grewal, *Retailing Management*, 11th ed. (McGraw-Hill)

Strategy-level. Section III "Merchandise Management":
- Ch 12 Managing the Merchandise Planning Process — types of merchandise management planning systems, forecasting category sales, control systems for managing inventory
- Ch 13 Buying Merchandise — vendor relationships, negotiation, terms of sale
- Ch 14 Retail Pricing — pricing strategy, markdowns at strategic level
- Ch 15 Retail Communication Mix

Use for: positioning aimily In-Season **vs** the existing retail-planning workflow taught in business schools (where it must integrate).

### 4.4 Lewis & Dart, *The New Rules of Retail* (Palgrave Macmillan, 2nd ed.)

Strategy book, not operational. Three-pillar framework:
1. **Neurological connectivity** — addictive customer experience
2. **Preemptive distribution** — all channels, ahead of competition
3. **Value-chain control** — vertical integration end-to-end

Useful for the **pitch deck** to senior retail executives (Mango, Inditex) — they read this book. Not useful for aimily In-Season's per-SKU decision logic.

## 5. What this tells us about aimily In-Season's decision taxonomy

### 5.1 Verbs that map cleanly (no changes needed)
- `Mantener` ↔ Maintain ✅
- `Markdown` ↔ Mark down (initial / second / terminal) ✅ — but expose the **markdown ladder step** explicitly
- `Reducir compra` ↔ Cancel / Reduce on Order ✅
- `Reponer N uds` ↔ Reorder / Replenish ✅
- `Matar` ↔ Discontinue / Exit / Kill ✅ — note "kill" is industry slang; classroom uses "discontinue"
- `Investigar` ↔ Investigate ✅ — but see split recommendation below

### 5.2 Verbs that need refinement
- **`Replicar estilo` + `Extender colores con swatches del moodboard`** ↔ "Reorder + Distort" + "Lean future OTB / next-season brief". The classroom canon is **Reorder + Distort** for current season and a separate **next-season carry-over** call. aimily's combined verb is a defensible UX choice but should be **labeled as two actions**: "amplify this season now (reorder + distort)" + "feed next season's brief". Two checkboxes, not one verb.
- **`Investigar`** should split into:
  - **Investigate** — cause unknown, variance is the signal, action is data-gathering
  - **Promote / Push** — cause known (soft sell-through, healthy margin, no markdown warranted), action is marketing lever (signage, email, placement)
  - This split mirrors what Donnellan Ch 12 calls "tactical price changes" vs "promotional support."

### 5.3 Verbs that are missing
- **`Transferir entre tiendas`** (Transfer between doors) — major in-season lever in multi-door retail (Zara, Mango, Inditex). Taught explicitly in FIT FM 423 allocation lab. **Recommend adding** for Mango / Inditex pitch.
- **`Esperar antes de bajar precio`** (Hold price; wait for plan-curve crossover) — explicit "do not markdown yet" verb. Currently aimily defaults to Maintain, which is ambiguous (could be "all good" or "we're watching"). Splitting "Maintain" into **Maintain (on plan)** vs **Hold (off plan but not yet actionable)** would mirror the buyer's actual mental model.

### 5.4 KPI surfacing gap
aimily In-Season's 6 confidence dimensions are sound for ML-stability scoring, but a senior US-trained buyer reading the output expects to see, at a glance:
1. Sell-through % vs plan-curve at week N
2. Weeks of cover vs lead time
3. GMROI (single number, target 3.0–3.5)
4. Stock-to-sales ratio (BOM / Sales)
5. Markdown % of sales
6. Maintained markup %

Adding these as the **headline buyer-readable row** above the confidence stack would close the "I trust this because it speaks my language" gap. The confidence stack is *secondary* — it's the ML trust layer, not the buyer trust layer.
