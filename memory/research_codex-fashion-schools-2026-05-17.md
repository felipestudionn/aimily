# Round 2 Research: Operational Craft Taught in Fashion-Retail Business Schools

## Executive Verdict

1. The most operational public curricula are **LCF/UAL, FIT, LIM, Parsons CPE, FIDM/ASU-FIDM, Fashion Retail Academy, and practitioner training firms**, not IESE/Bocconi/ESADE/IE-style MBA programs. The latter teach fashion strategy, luxury, go-to-market, retail operations, and cases, but they do not publicly expose Monday-morning trading mechanics.

2. The classroom backbone is not “AI forecasting.” It is **retail math + six-month merchandise plan + OTB/WSSI + sell-through + markdown control + vendor negotiation**. FIT’s `FM 203`, `FM 206`, `FM 224/225` legacy sequence is the cleanest US canonical path.

3. **LCF’s PG Cert Fashion Buying and Merchandising** is closest to the operational role Felipe described: 15 weeks, business numeracy, buying/merchandising integration, range planning, pricing, sourcing, stock movement, merchandising data, and a simulated buying negotiation scenario. Public page names Helen Hindley as course leader and Nicole Dunlop as programme director; Dunlop’s bio says she began in Jaeger buying and became sourcing manager at Adams.

4. **Public reading lists are mostly not available.** Schools publish course names and outcomes, but not course packs. Where reading lists surface, the recurring texts are Goworek, Donnellan, Tepper/Greene, Diamond/Pintel or Clodfelter, Levy/Weitz, and case packs from HBS/IESE/Ivey/Bocconi.

5. The most actionable book for Monday-morning mechanics is **Angella Hoffman, _Fundamentals of Merchandising Math and Retail Buying_**, because its table of contents explicitly includes **Sell Thru**, **Stock-to-Sales Analysis**, **Six-Month Merchandise Plan**, **Buying Simulations**, **End-of-Season Negotiations**, **Monday Morning Reports: Sales**, and **Monday Morning Reports: Markdowns**.

6. The European “WSSI” tradition and the US “Six-Month Plan / OTB” tradition are the same operating loop in different dialects. WSSI is weekly sales, stock and intake; OTB is the buyable budget left after target closing stock, planned sales, markdowns, opening stock and committed intake.

7. Schools rarely publish fixed sell-through thresholds. Public evidence supports teaching the **decision method**, not universal cutoffs: compare actual vs plan, rate of sale, weeks cover, full-price sell-through, markdown exposure, stock by size/store/channel, intake commitments, and margin impact. Any threshold library for aimily must be configurable by category, lifecycle, price architecture, and replenishment lead time.

8. Mango-specific school leverage is strongest through **IESE**, not because IESE publishes operational merchandising courses, but because Mango has direct institutional ties: Isak Andic was on IESE’s International Advisory Board, Mango sponsors/has inaugurated a Mango Classroom at IESE New York, and Toni Ruiz/Isak Andic lectured in the Executive MBA. ESADE has hosted Toni Ruiz at Matins Esade.

9. Inditex/Zara “commercials” are the human archetype aimily should model: cross-functional HQ-side decision makers around product, store feedback, sales signal, replenishment, scarcity, and rapid assortment correction. The best cited operational source remains Caro & Gallien’s Zara inventory work plus HBS/IESE Zara cases, but the human training curriculum is not public.

10. Product implication: aimily In-Season should be framed as **a Monday-morning trading copilot**, not a forecasting dashboard. Its first job is to turn sell-through reports into a disciplined decision packet: classify item, diagnose cause, quantify OTB/WSSI impact, recommend action, assign owner, and carry learning into post-season review.

## Per-School Deep Dive

### 1. London College of Fashion, UAL: PG Cert Fashion Buying and Merchandising / BA Fashion Buying and Merchandising

**Public evidence level:** strong for course structure, moderate for faculty, weak for reading list.

UAL’s PG Cert is the most operationally explicit European course I found. It is a **15-week full-time course** split into three units. UAL says it prepares students for entry positions in buying and merchandising departments and uses industry simulation and guest speakers. The public curriculum is unusually close to the buyer/merchandiser operating loop.

**Course-level curriculum:**

- **Unit 1: Buying and Merchandising.** Covers integration of buying, merchandising, design and garment technology; commercially balanced range planning; lead times; pricing strategies; sourcing strategies; stock movement; and use of merchandising data in range plan development.
- **Unit 2: Marketing and Supply Chain Management.** Covers the market context for buyers and merchandisers, commercial implications for product ranges, typical supply chain for a large multiple fashion retailer, and multichannel marketing mixes.
- **Unit 3: Integrated Project.** A five-week applied project around a complex buying scenario, including problem solving, negotiation, team roles, and a hypothetical retailer-supplier negotiation.

**Operational artifacts implied:**

- Range plan.
- Pricing architecture.
- Sourcing strategy.
- Stock movement / stock flow view.
- Merchandising-data-led range decision.
- Supplier negotiation scenario.
- Log book / reflective analysis.

**Faculty crossover:**

- **Helen Hindley** is listed as course leader.
- **Nicole Dunlop**, Programme Director Product and Innovation, is described by UAL as having started in Jaeger’s buying team and later becoming sourcing manager for Adams. That is relevant industry-side buying/sourcing crossover.

**Reading lists:** not public on the course page. Educated inference: likely uses Goworek and UK retail buying/merchandising texts, but I would not encode that as fact without syllabus access.

**Weekly trading ritual evidence:** the PG Cert page does not say “weekly trading meeting,” but it explicitly teaches merchandising data, stock movement, pricing, range plan, and buying scenario negotiation. That is enough to infer it trains the ingredients of the ritual, not enough to claim a named Monday meeting simulation.

Sources: [UAL PG Cert Fashion Buying and Merchandising](https://www.arts.ac.uk/subjects/fashion-business/postgraduate/pg-cert-fashion-buying-and-merchandising-lcf), [UAL BA Fashion Buying and Merchandising](https://www.arts.ac.uk/fashion/courses/undergraduate/ba-fashion-buying-and-merchandising/)

## Top 5 US Programs

### 1. Fashion Institute of Technology: Fashion Business Management

**Public evidence level:** very strong for course catalog, weak for reading lists.

FIT is the cleanest public US canon for retail buying/planning education.

**Key current courses:**

- **FM 203: Business Intelligence in Planning and Buying**, 3 credits. Teaches business analytics used by merchants, product developers, planners and wholesale sales; students measure and analyze financial KPIs used in fashion business.
- **FM 206: Strategies of Merchandising**, 3 credits. AAS capstone; students develop merchandise strategies to buy, plan and merchandise a retail business in stores and online; students actively develop merchandise plans using industry analytics.
- **FM 207: Retail Strategies**, 3 credits. Retail landscape, channel classification, data analytics, financial strategy, supply chain structures and retail management.
- **FM 222: Import Buying**, 3 credits. Import programs, product distribution, market sources and financing.
- **FM 245 / equivalent forecasting course:** trend and forecast research methods for planning, purchasing, or merchandising fashion product lines.

**Legacy course names that map to the older canon:**

- **FM 224: Merchandising Math Applications.** FIT’s past catalog says students learn calculations needed to interpret and respond to financial planning and control reports, including sales analyses, maintained markup reports, gross margin, and seasonal plans.
- **FM 225: Fashion Merchandising.** Functions/objectives of buying, planning, product development and account executives; team projects to execute a seasonal plan.
- **FM 322: Fashion Inventory Management** appears in older catalogs.

**Reading list:** not public in FIT catalog. Likely use of retail math/buying textbooks is an inference.

**Fit for aimily:** strongest US source for encoding the six-month plan / KPI / merchandise-plan logic.

Source: [FIT Fashion Business Management courses](https://catalog.fitnyc.edu/undergraduate/courses/fm/)

## Mango Campus and Inditex Training

### Mango

**What is public:**

- Mango HQ is in **Palau-solità i Plegamans**, Barcelona, centralizing activity and managing operations worldwide.
- The HQ includes the **Hangar Design Centre**, with more than 500 people in product/design, designing more than 18,000 garments and accessories annually.
- Mango is building **Campus Mango**, planned to house more than 2,200 employees.
- Mango’s 2025 employer release says it provided **587,000 hours of training and internal courses**.
- Mango launched **Craft Your Future** with LCI Barcelona, a five-month subsidized program around pattern making, industrial garment construction, digital prototyping and quality control.

**What is not public:**

- I found no public syllabus for Mango internal buyer, planner, commercial or in-season trading training.
- No public Mango WSSI/OTB templates.

## Reconstructed Monday-Morning Ritual

**Evidence basis:** cited course materials and practitioner training mention Monday reports, WSSI, OTB, sell-through, markdowns, trading routines, retail math and merchandising data. The sequence below is an inference, but it is consistent with the classroom artifacts.

### Attendees

- Buyer / assistant buyer.
- Merchandiser / merchandise planner.
- Allocator / replenishment analyst.
- Category manager / head of buying.
- E-commerce trading lead.
- Store operations or area manager.
- Marketing/promotions when action requires traffic.
- Supply chain/logistics if intake or DC constraints matter.
- Finance/controller when OTB, margin or markdown budget shifts.

### Inputs

- Last week sales by SKU/style/color/size/channel/store.
- WTD/MTD/STD season-to-date vs plan, LY and forecast.
- Sell-through and full-price sell-through.
- Rate of sale.
- Stock on hand and weeks cover.
- Broken sizes and stockout report.
- Intake due, purchase orders, cancellations and delays.
- OTB / WSSI position.
- Markdown list and markdown budget.
- Returns, especially online.
- Store qualitative feedback.
- Competitor/trend signal.
- Weather/local event impact.

### Agenda

1. **Topline check:** sales, margin, stock and markdown vs plan.
2. **Category heatmap:** departments/classes with over/under-trade.
3. **Bestsellers:** determine if true demand or merely over-distributed; check sizes, channels, stores, margin and replenishment lead time.
4. **Worst-sellers:** diagnose product, price, placement, size curve, channel, launch timing, marketing visibility or weather.
5. **Stock risk:** stockouts, broken sizes, excess cover, terminal-stock risk.
6. **Intake review:** what is arriving, late, cancelable, deferrable or acceleratable.
7. **OTB/WSSI review:** whether new buy budget exists; whether it should be moved between categories.
8. **Markdown/promotional decisions:** depth, timing, exclusions, channel scope.
9. **Allocation/reallocation:** move stock to where rate of sale is strongest.
10. **Action log:** decision, owner, financial impact, deadline.
11. **Learning capture:** what this implies for next season’s range, depth, fit, price, color, delivery phasing.

### Decision Outputs

- Rebuy/repeat.
- Chase/expedite.
- Cancel/reduce future intake.
- Reallocate/transfer.
- Replenish stores/channels.
- Markdown now/later/deeper.
- Promote/feature.
- Hold price and monitor.
- Feed learning into next season.

## OTB and Six-Month Plan Mechanics

### Six-Month Plan Structure

The US classroom canon treats the six-month plan as a pre-season financial map with monthly control.

Typical rows:

- Planned sales.
- Planned markdowns.
- Planned beginning-of-month stock.
- Planned end-of-month stock.
- Planned purchases / receipts.
- Initial markup.
- Maintained markup.
- Gross margin.
- Stock-to-sales ratio.
- Turnover.
- OTB.

Typical process:

1. Review LY and trend.
2. Set sales plan by month/class/channel.
3. Set stock-to-sales or weeks-cover targets.
4. Set planned markdowns.
5. Calculate required purchases.
6. Approve budget.
7. Convert budget into classification/assortment plan.
8. Track actuals weekly and revise.

### OTB Formula

A common retail formula, also reflected in WSSI training language:

`OTB = target closing stock + planned sales + planned markdowns - opening stock - committed intake`

At weekly level, WSSI turns this into a rolling control:

- Opening stock.
- Sales.
- Intake/receipts.
- Markdown.
- Closing stock.
- Cover.
- OTB.

### Operational Trigger Logic

Schools and public training rarely publish fixed thresholds, but they teach the control logic:

- If sales exceed plan and cover is falling: chase/rebuy/expedite/reallocate.
- If sales exceed plan but stock is already committed elsewhere: prioritize stores/channels by rate of sale.
- If sell-through lags and cover rises: diagnose cause, then markdown/promote/cancel intake.
- If markdown budget is overused: protect full-price winners and cut dogs earlier.
- If terminal stock is above target: accelerate clearance.
- If key sizes are broken: replenish or transfer before declaring weak demand.

## Markdown Decision Flow

**Classroom method, not universal thresholds:**

1. Identify slow sellers vs plan.
2. Separate “bad product” from “bad distribution” and “bad visibility.”
3. Check age, seasonality, weeks cover, size curve, return rate and margin.
4. Review future intake and cancelability.
5. Choose action:
   - Hold if early and signal is inconclusive.
   - Promote if visibility/traffic problem.
   - Reallocate if geography/channel mismatch.
   - First markdown if demand is structurally below plan.
   - Deeper markdown if first cut fails or terminal stock risk remains.
   - Clearance/outlet if end-of-season.
6. Quantify gross margin impact and cash released.
7. Feed into post-season: overbuy, wrong price, wrong color, bad fit, bad timing, wrong store cluster.

**Aimily implication:** markdown recommendation should never be naked. It should say “markdown because X, not Y,” and show why non-price actions are insufficient.

## Hero / Dog / Star / Dead Classification

A practical classification engine should combine:

| Class | Signal | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Hero / winner | High sell-through, high rate of sale, good margin, low returns, broad store/channel adoption, size curve healthy | Rebuy, chase, protect full price, expand allocation |
| Star but constrained | High demand but stockout/broken sizes suppress sales | Replenish, transfer, prioritize scarce inventory, repeat |
| Cash cow/basic | Stable sales, predictable replenishment, lower trend risk | Maintain cover, automate replenishment, avoid unnecessary markdown |
| Question mark | Early read, mixed signals by store/channel/size | Monitor, test exposure, small reallocation, avoid premature markdown |
| Dog | Low sell-through, weak ROS, high cover, no clear distribution issue | Markdown, cancel intake, reduce future depth |
| Dead stock | Aged, low/no sales after action, terminal risk | Clearance/outlet/liquidation |

The key classroom correction for aimily: **a SKU can look like a dog because it is broken in key sizes.** Zara’s display-removal logic makes this explicit.
