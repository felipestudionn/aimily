# Practitioner-to-Classroom Layer: Books, Corporate Academies, and Operational Vocabulary for Fashion Buying & Merchandising

> Research output for grounding **aimily In-Season** in how working buyers / merchandisers / commercials actually think and talk. Pilot target: **Mango**. Pipeline: Mango → Tendam → El Corte Inglés → midmarket EU.
>
> Date: 2026-05-17 · Scope: 5 pillars (practitioner books · Mango Campus · Inditex training · industry programs · trade press deep dives) · Spanish-language vocabulary called out separately.

---

## Executive Summary — 10 Hard Bullets

1. **The Monday Trade Meeting is the universal operational ritual.** Every UK / EU / US fashion retailer above ~£50M revenue runs a Monday-morning trading meeting where buyers, merchandisers, planners and (often) trading director review the previous week's sales vs. budget, stock-vs-sales variance, weeks-of-cover, sell-through and aged inventory — and emit decisions (reorder · markdown · cancel · accelerate intake · push allocation · consolidate). This rhythm is documented in Drapers profiles (Linda Quinn at JD Williams "Mondays are about trading"; Suzanne Bailey at Mothercare), and the operational structure is shipped as canonical templates by Style Arcade (built by ex-buyers) and the Fashion Retail Academy's WSSI short course.

2. **WSSI ("wizzy") is the canonical control surface** — Weekly Sales, Stock and Intake. It is industry-standard across UK/EU. Aimily In-Season decision verbs should map onto WSSI vocabulary, not generic statistical labels. The merchandiser "trades" the department through the WSSI. The Fashion Retail Academy in London teaches a dedicated WSSI short course; Retail Assist, First Friday, PPN Solutions all sell WSSI products to mid-market retailers. **This is a Mango-friendly vocabulary** — same as what a buyer's tools already use.

3. **Saviolo & Corbellini (Bocconi) is the canonical European fashion-management textbook**, used at Bocconi MAFED, Coursera "Management of Fashion and Luxury Companies", and as required reading in IESE/IE executive education. Chapters 7, 8, 12, 13 cover product strategy, retail management, communication and the wholesale/retail process — the exact arc aimily In-Season operates inside. *Sales/buying weekly mechanics are NOT covered* in the Bocconi books, which confirms our prior finding: academic canon describes strategy, not in-season firefighting.

4. **The Caro & Gallien "commercial" role at Zara is the operational archetype Mango wants to match.** Caro & Gallien (2010) documented that the formal Zara process for weekly store shipments uses optimization models executed by *"commercials"* — HQ-side staff who sit between product managers and stores, decide weekly shipment quantities, and react to stockout signals. Sales lift was 3-4%. **Mango does not have this role institutionalized.** This is aimily's wedge.

5. **Inditex Go! program (2013-present) is Inditex's internal "commercial" academy.** Personalised development plan revolving around stores, 6+ months rotating through procurement, distribution, management control, logistics. Originally Zara-only; expanded to Massimo Dutti, Zara Home, Oysho, Pull&Bear. **Mango has no equivalent program with that depth.** Their CSDMM-UPM partnership (2024) is sustainability-only training, not commercial decision-making.

6. **The practitioner books that matter for aimily are 4, not 14**: Goworek "Fashion Buying" (Wiley, 2007, 2e — Leeds Beckett faculty, ex-buyer; gives the buying-cycle canonical structure), Jackson & Shaw "Mastering Fashion Buying and Merchandising Management" (Palgrave, 2009, 2e — LCF faculty, both ex-Marks & Spencer / Burton; the European reference textbook), Shaw & Koumbis "Fashion Buying: From Trend Forecasting to Shop Floor" (Bloomsbury Basics, 2017; rewritten 2024 by McTurk & Koumbis — LIM College faculty, ex-Urban Outfitters), and Kincade & Gibson "Merchandising of Fashion Products" (Prentice Hall, 2010 — Virginia Tech, US perspective with Six Rights / model stock budgets). The Bocconi / INSEAD books (Saviolo, Corbellini, Godart, Marazza, Pinkhasov) are strategy / brand level — useful for the *Mango buyer's MBA* but not for *aimily's decision verbs*.

7. **BoF Education's "Art and Science of Buying and Merchandising"** (Susanne Tide-Frater, 8 hours video, self-directed) is the single most current practitioner-authored course online. Tide-Frater: 20+ years at Selfridges, Harrods, Victoria Beckham, brand strategy director at Farfetch. The course explicitly teaches *how buyers and merchandisers mix instinct with hard data* — this is the wedge aimily occupies (giving the merchandiser a defensible *data-side* of that instinct/data marriage).

8. **WGSN now ships TrendCurve AI assortment-planning into the buyer's chair**, claiming 94% trend forecast accuracy and offering an "Opportunity Calculator" that quantifies white space vs. competitor benchmark. **This is the closest competitor to aimily In-Season at the buyer's desk.** WGSN is *forecast-side* (what to back next season); aimily is *forensic-side* (what last season tells us). The two are complements, but if Mango already pays WGSN, they will want aimily to integrate, not compete.

9. **The Spanish-language vocabulary is shallow on operational terms** — "comprador" is generic, "merchandiser" is loaned from English, no widely-used Spanish equivalent for "trading meeting" (it's used as-is, English) or "open-to-buy" (sometimes "presupuesto abierto"). Inditex uses *"comerciales"* (commercials) which is the canonical Spanish industry word for the Zara HQ role. **Mango uses Spanglish on the inside.** WSSI is pronounced "wizzy" in both English and Spanish. Aimily UI should be EN+ES dual, with English KPIs untranslated (sell-through, weeks of cover, intake, WSSI, OTB) because that's how Spanish merchandisers speak.

10. **Trade press operationally-rigorous deep dives are concentrated in 4 sources**: Drapers (UK, weekly, subscriber, daily-life of buyer profiles + trading-director roundtables + supply-chain summit white papers) · Modaes (Spain, daily, partial paywall, the canonical Mango/Inditex executive-movement source) · Vogue Business (global, since 2019, increasingly operational) · BoF Insights (paid reports, McKinsey-grade depth on Mango/Zara/Shein). Sourcing Journal and Just-Style are supply-chain-side, less buyer-side. **For Mango pitch credibility, cite Modaes first, BoF Insights second, Drapers third.**

## Per-Book Profiles (8 books most operationally relevant)

### 1. Goworek, Helen — *Fashion Buying* (2e, Wiley-Blackwell, 2007; ISBN 9781405149921)

- **Author background**: Senior Lecturer in Marketing, Leeds Beckett University (formerly Leeds Met). Ex-buyer at major UK fashion retailers before academia (industry experience cited in foreword). Author of multiple academic articles on UK fashion buying as practice.
- **Scope**: 12 chapters + glossary. The European/UK canon for the buying-cycle structure.
- **Operationally relevant chapters**:
  - Ch.2 "The Role of the Fashion Buyer" — buyer qualities, liaising with suppliers and internal departments (design, merchandising, QA), negotiation. The discipline-boundary chapter.
  - Ch.3 "The Buying Cycle" — review of current season's sales, budget planning, comparative shopping, directional shopping, range planning and selection, critical path. *This is the canonical 6-stage buying cycle that the Spanish word "ciclo de compras" maps to.*
  - Ch.5 "Range Planning" — compiling a range plan, seasons and phases, sales history, options and colourways, sizing, calculating retail prices, classifying fashion merchandise within the range plan, factors affecting range performance. **Aimily In-Season's "extend colors" recommendation maps directly to Goworek Ch.5 "options and colourways".**
  - Ch.10 "Buying Branded Fashion Merchandise" — budget planning and ordering, processing orders and monitoring deliveries, monitoring sales figures and reviewing the season's performance. **This is where the post-mortem ritual lives in the textbook.**
- **Verdict**: Most-cited in UK academic syllabi. Practitioner-pedagogical (Goworek was a buyer). Reference for *vocabulary, not decision math*.

### 2. Jackson, Tim & Shaw, David — *Mastering Fashion Buying and Merchandising Management* (2e, Palgrave Macmillan, 2009 / 2017; ISBN 9780333801659 / 9781137609113)

- **Author backgrounds**: 
  - **Tim Jackson** — Senior Lecturer at London College of Fashion (UAL). Ex-Marks & Spencer buyer (12+ years), now consulting. LinkedIn confirms LCF affiliation; cited as the European reference author.
  - **David Shaw** — Visiting Lecturer LCF; ex-Burton Group merchandiser. Co-author of *The Fashion Handbook* (Routledge, 2006) and *Fashion Buying: From Trend Forecasting to Shop Floor* (Bloomsbury, 2017).
- **Scope**: "First academic textbook covering European retail fashion buying and merchandising" per the publisher. 10 chapters in 1e; 2e adds Trends, Range Planning, Skills for Buyers and Merchandisers chapters and two glossaries.
- **Operationally relevant content**:
  - Distinguishes buyer (creative + commercial, decides *what* to buy) from merchandiser (financial + analytical, decides *how much* and *when*). **The single best operational definition of the discipline boundary.**
  - Ch.10 "Future trends in buying and merchandising" — fashion buyer/merchandiser of the future, IT impact on retail.
  - Range Planning chapter (2e) — the canonical mechanism that aimily's "extend / kill / amplify" decisions reverse-engineer.
  - Glossary is the EU-recognised vocabulary list (intake, cover, WSSI, OTB, ASP, sell-through).
- **Verdict**: **The reference book to align aimily's verbs with.** Jackson is at LCF, where Mango sends its talent (via CSDMM-UPM Madrid partnership). If aimily speaks Jackson-Shaw vocabulary, Mango buyers will recognise it as native.

### 3. Shaw, David & Koumbis, Dimitri — *Fashion Buying: From Trend Forecasting to Shop Floor* (Bloomsbury Basics Fashion Management, 2017 1e; McTurk & Koumbis 2e 2024; ISBN 9781474252928 / 9781350280571)

- **Author backgrounds**:
  - **Dimitri Koumbis** — Lecturer at LIM College NYC. 15+ years retail (Urban Outfitters, Gap Inc.), now consultant for emerging brands.
  - **Clare McTurk** (2e) — Industry practitioner currently writing the 2024 revision.
- **Scope**: 5 chapters (1e). Pragmatic introduction with industry case studies and practitioner interviews.
- **Operationally relevant chapters**:
  - Ch.1 "The Fashion Buyer" — buying teams, working environment, fashion buying approaches and consumer, fashion cycles, product-specific purchasing classifications. **Case study: The Global Buying Director (interview with Andrew Searson, Adidas).**
  - Ch.2 "Sources of Buying Inspiration" — market intelligence, trend forecasting, buying markets and international trade fairs, competition shopping. Case study: Seek Berlin at Berlin Fashion Week; interview Damien Winpenny.
  - Ch.3 "Suppliers, Sourcing, Communication and Range Planning" — buyer-supplier relationship, sourcing suppliers, managing the supply base.
  - **Ch.4 "Range and Merchandise Planning" — merchandise planning concepts, the buyer-merchandiser relationship, *buyer instinct vs. planning*, KPIs, merchandise planning processes, pricing and markdowns, range balance. This is the chapter aimily In-Season disrupts. The book calls it "instinct vs planning"; aimily provides the third pole: forensic evidence.**
  - Ch.5 "Trends in Fashion Buying" — branding, advertising, social media, visual merchandising, fashion technology, CSR, fashion circularity, IP rights.
- **Verdict**: Most US-friendly of the books. LIM College graduates from this book; many of them become buyers at US mid-market retailers Mango is trying to enter (Anthropologie, J.Crew, Nordstrom).

### 4. Kincade, Doris H. & Gibson, Fay Y. — *Merchandising of Fashion Products* (Prentice Hall, 2010; ISBN 9780131731257)

- **Author backgrounds**: Both **Virginia Tech (Apparel, Housing & Resource Management dept)**. Kincade is widely cited in the academic merchandising literature; Gibson has 30+ years of retail practice background (cited in the textbook). They also wrote *Merchandising Math: A Managerial Approach* (Prentice Hall, 2003).
- **Scope**: US-centric, comprehensive merchandising process from strategic planning to product delivery.
- **Operationally relevant chapters**:
  - "Retail buyers prepare for market" — financial planning, **buying-selling curve** (the canonical US visualization of timing), six-month merchandise budgets, **model stock budgets**, product planning, the **Six Rights of Merchandising** (right product, place, time, quantity, price, customer — the operational mantra).
  - "Retail buyers shop the market" — fashion weeks, geographic market centers, vendor selection, writing purchase orders, paying invoices, negotiating with vendors.
  - "Retail buyers translate market findings" — presentation techniques, retail environment and image, merchandise presentation, promotions, publicity, packaging.
- **Verdict**: Best US-side complement. The "Six Rights of Merchandising" mantra is universal (also used at Mango per their visual-merchandising training material) and is the simplest decision-tree compass for explaining what aimily In-Season optimizes against.

### 5. Saviolo, Stefania & Testa, Salvo — *Strategic Management in the Fashion Companies* / *Le imprese del sistema moda* (ETAS Libri Milan, 2002 / 2005; ISBN 9788845309946)

- **Author backgrounds**: Both **SDA Bocconi School of Management, Milan**. Saviolo founded MAFED (Master in Fashion, Experience & Design Management) at Bocconi. Testa academic + consultant. Saviolo is the most-cited European fashion management academic.
- **Scope**: Italian fashion industry as system. Chapters on strategy, product, retail, distribution, communication, brand.
- **Operationally relevant content**: 
  - "Le strategie di prodotto e il Merchandising Plan" — product strategy + merchandising plan
  - "Il piano di merchandising e il processo di sviluppo del prodotto" — merchandising plan + product development process
  - "Il processo commerciale: gestione dei canali wholesale and retail" — wholesale/retail channel management.
- **Verdict**: Strategy-tier, not desk-side. **Cite as credibility-anchor when pitching Mango executives** — Saviolo is *the* Italian fashion academic — but don't expect operational decision verbs from this book.

### 6. Corbellini, Erica & Saviolo, Stefania — *Managing Fashion and Luxury Companies* (ETAS, 2009; ISBN 9788817061650)

- **Author backgrounds**: Erica Paola Corbellini — Professor of Management, Bocconi + SDA Bocconi MAFED faculty. Coursera instructor (Bocconi "Management of Fashion and Luxury Companies" MOOC).
- **Scope**: 14 chapters, used in MAFED + Coursera + IESE/IE executive education. Translated to Japanese, Chinese.
- **Chapter authorship split** (per publisher):
  - **Corbellini**: Ch.3, 4 (w/ D'Souza), 6, 8, 13, plus sections 1.4, 2.4, 11.4, 12.1, 12.4, 12.6, 14.4
  - **Saviolo**: Ch.1, 2, 5, 7, 9, 10, 11, 12, 14 + sections
- **Bocconi MAFED required chapters**: 1, 2, 3 (excl. 3.5), 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 12, 13, 14 (chs 4, 5, 11 excluded)
- **Operationally relevant**: Ch.12 (Retail) and Ch.13 (Communication and Distribution) — channel and retail-format strategy. **No weekly trading mechanics.**
- **Verdict**: This is the book a senior Mango buyer has on their MBA shelf. Quote it for credibility framing; do not expect it to teach the Monday-morning meeting.

### 7. Lewis, Robin & Dart, Michael — *The New Rules of Retail* (Palgrave Macmillan, 2010 / 2014; ISBN 9780230105720 / 9781137279262)

- **Author backgrounds**: 
  - **Robin Lewis** — former Executive Editor of *Women's Wear Daily* (WWD); 40+ years writing on US retail; runs "The Robin Report" trade newsletter.
  - **Michael Dart** — Partner at Kurt Salmon (consultant, ex-Nike, ex-Coach).
- **Scope**: US retail thesis: 3-4 waves from producer power → demand creation → consumer power → Jobsian/Bezosian era. Chapters on Apple, Walmart, Sears as case studies; specialty apparel chains as the "master model".
- **Operationally relevant**: "Preemptive Distribution", "Neurological Connectivity", "Value Chain Control" — strategic, not desk-side. The Robin Lewis case-study sections on Limited Brands, Coach, Ralph Lauren contain operational anecdote.
- **Verdict**: Strategic context for US pitches. Less directly operational than Goworek/Jackson/Shaw.

### 8. Godart, Frédéric — *Unveiling Fashion: Business, Culture, and Identity in the Most Glamorous Industry* (Palgrave Macmillan / INSEAD Business Press, 2012; ISBN 9780230358355)

- **Author background**: Assistant Professor of Organisational Behaviour at INSEAD Fontainebleau; PhD sociology, Columbia. The "fashion sociologist" of the European business-school world.
- **Scope**: 6 principles — Affirmation, Convergence, Autonomy, Personalization, Symbolization, Imperialization.
- **Operationally relevant**: None directly. Useful for the sociology of *why* trend cycles work the way they do. Worth citing in aimily's "why archetypes matter" framing.
- **Verdict**: Background reading only. Not operational.

## Inditex Training Profile — How Zara Trains Commercials and Buyers

### The Inditex Go! Program (2013-present)
- Created 2013 for **Zara** initially, **expanded to Zara Home, Massimo Dutti, Oysho, Pull&Bear** (procurement, distribution, management control, logistics).
- **Purpose**: Identify promising in-store talent → promote to HQ commercial / buying / merchandising roles. Bottom-up talent pipeline.
- **Structure**: "Personalized development plan revolving around the stores" — 6+ months rotating between store and HQ functions.
- **Outcome statistic**: 80% of graduate program participants continue with Inditex teams (per Inditex 2017 annual report).
- This program is the institutional answer to "how do you make a 25-year-old store associate ready to run weekly shipment decisions for a country's worth of Zara stores".

### Inditex Graduate Program / Business
- Training duration: **2 weeks to 3 months** depending on position. Rotation through production, distribution, stock control, people management, communication, marketing, **buying**, **commercial operations**, in-store training.
- Eligible: final-year university students.
- The "commercials" role specifically (the HQ-side person Caro & Gallien 2010 documented) sits inside the **Inditex People Business** vertical.

### The "Commercials" Role (per Caro & Gallien 2010 and public Inditex job descriptions)
- HQ-based, country-team-organised. Each commercial owns a country's allocation for a subset of product categories.
- Decision rights: weekly shipment quantities per store per SKU, in-season repeat orders, kill decisions, accelerated markdown decisions.
- Tools: Inditex's proprietary optimization model (Caro/Gallien algorithm: 3-4% sales lift documented), real-time POS feed from every store, daily product-manager handoffs.
- **Reports to**: country commercial director / product director.

### Inditex's central locations
- **Arteixo** (A Coruña, Galicia) — Inditex HQ, Zara central design + Zara.com hub (67,000 m² inaugurated 2021, €130M). The "school" environment for Zara product teams.
- **Sabón** (Arteixo) — Zara España logistics & online cuartel.
- **Barcelona** — multiple brand HQs.
- **Alicante** — Tempe (footwear arm) HQ.

### Inditex partnerships with business schools (public record)
- IESE Business School — multiple case studies on Inditex; Inditex executives lecture at IESE Fashion & Luxury Goods Industry Meeting (annual, 16+ editions).
- Universidad de A Coruña — community partnership, but no documented buyer-specific training program.
- No formal partnership for *teaching Inditex commercials* — they train internally.

### Books / publications from ex-Inditex public commentators
- **Covadonga O'Shea** — "The Man from Zara: The Story of the Genius Behind the Inditex Group" (LID Editorial, 2008; English 2012; ISBN 9781907794216). Biographical, executive-level, light on operational mechanics but the canonical Amancio Ortega narrative.
- **Felipe Caro** (UCLA Anderson) — academic chapter "Fast Fashion: Business Model Overview and Research Opportunities" (Caro & Martínez-de-Albéniz, 2014). With **Jérémie Gallien** (London Business School): "Inventory Management of a Fast-Fashion Retail Network" (2010), "Zara Uses Operations Research to Reengineer Its Global Distribution Process" (Interfaces, 2010). **These are the academic-but-operational sources for how the commercial role really works at Zara.**

### Implication for aimily pitch
- Inditex spends 7-figures per year training "commercials" — and even then runs an optimization model on top.
- Mango's commercials, if they exist, are not formally trained.
- aimily In-Season is the **forensic layer Mango can add without standing up a Go!-equivalent program**.

## The Operational Vocabulary — Distilled From Practitioners (NOT Academics, NOT Vendors)

### Universal English (used at every UK/EU retailer, including Mango internally in Spanglish)

| Term | Meaning | Where it appears |
|---|---|---|
| **WSSI** ("wizzy") | Weekly Sales, Stock and Intake. The control document for the merchandise department. | FRA, Drapers, Style Arcade, every UK retailer |
| **OTB** | Open-to-Buy. The budget remaining for in-season purchases. | Every retailer; Mango internal |
| **Sell-through** | % of stock sold in a given period (units sold / units received) | Universal |
| **Weeks of cover** / **forward cover** | How many weeks of sales current stock represents | Universal |
| **Intake** | Inbound stock arriving from suppliers (units or value) | Universal |
| **Trade meeting** / **Monday trade** | The weekly review-and-decide ritual | Drapers, Style Arcade, Mothercare profile, JD Williams profile |
| **CAYG** | Clear As You Go — early markdown discipline | Style Arcade, UK retailers |
| **Aged inventory** | Stock past target-weeks-on-floor threshold | Universal |
| **Markdown** / **rebaja** | Permanent price reduction | Universal |
| **Allocation** / **push allocation** | Sending stock to specific stores/channels | Universal |
| **Reorder** / **repeat** | In-season reorder of an already-bought style | Universal |
| **Replenishment** | Continuous restock of a basic/never-out-of-stock SKU | Universal |
| **Phase** | Sub-season delivery wave (e.g. SS27 P1, P2, P3) | Goworek Ch.5, UK retailers |
| **Range plan** | The pre-season blueprint of what will be bought, in what quantities, by phase | Universal |
| **Critical path** | The pre-season project plan from design brief to shop floor | Goworek Ch.3, universal |
| **Comp shop** / **directional shop** | Visit competitor stores to assess; visit trend-leader stores to inspire | Goworek, universal |
| **Six Rights** | Right product · right place · right time · right quantity · right price · right customer | Kincade, universal |
| **Buying cycle** | The 12-stage repeating annual structure | Goworek Ch.3 |
| **ASP** | Average Selling Price | Universal |
| **GMROI** | Gross Margin Return On Inventory | Kincade, US-leaning |
| **Stock-to-sales ratio** | Inventory units vs sales velocity ratio | WSSI core metric |
| **Best seller** / **worst seller** | The two ends of every Monday trade meeting | Universal |
| **Newness** | New product just launched / about to launch | Style Arcade, UK retailers |
| **Wardrobing** | Customer buys, wears, returns. The dark side of returns. | Drapers returns coverage |

## The Monday-Morning Ritual, Reconstructed From Cited Sources

Based on Style Arcade's working-merchandiser template, Drapers profiles (Quinn, Bailey, Tak), and FRA WSSI short course:

**07:00-08:30 — Pre-meeting prep (analyst/junior merchandiser)**
- Pull WSSI snapshot: actual sales vs forecast for prior week (units + £, by category + channel + brand)
- Pull product-performance table: top 20 best sellers, bottom 20 worst sellers, filtered by sell-through and gross margin
- Pull stock-vs-sales variance: which categories are overbought, which are understocked
- Pull aged-inventory flag list: SKUs past their target weeks-on-floor
- Pull intake pipeline: what's arriving this week, next 4 weeks
- Pull returns rate change vs prior week
- Pull competitor flag (if Edited/Retviews subscription): any major moves overnight

**09:00-10:30 — Trade meeting**
- **Attendees**: Trading director (chair), buying director, merchandise director, planning director, e-commerce director, retail/store-ops director, allocation manager, sometimes design director, sometimes marketing director. At mid-size retailers (Mango scale): ~8-15 people.
- **Agenda** (canonical, ~90 min):
  1. Week N-1 sales vs budget (5 min, summary chart)
  2. Channel deep-dive: ecom vs store (10 min)
  3. Category-by-category review (30 min — biggest block): each buyer/merchandiser presents their category's WSSI status, surfaces best/worst sellers, proposes actions
  4. Stock health review: aged inventory, stock-to-sales ratio anomalies (10 min)
  5. Intake pipeline + supplier delivery status (10 min)
  6. Decisions log: reorder list, markdown list, allocation moves, cancellations (10 min)
  7. Next-week priority and risks (5 min)
- **Decisions emitted**:
  - **Reorder** specific SKUs (in-season repeat purchase order — requires OTB authorization)
  - **Markdown** specific SKUs at specific % (permission to drop from 0% to 20%, 30%, 50%)
  - **Cancel** open purchase orders that have lost relevance
  - **Accelerate intake** (call supplier, pull forward)
  - **Push allocation** (move stock from one channel/region/store-cluster to another)
  - **Consolidate** (merge two clusters' allocations)
  - **Hold** (delay launch / extend life of a hero)
  - **Investigate** (data anomaly — defer decision pending diagnosis)
  - **Replicate** (apply learning from winner X to design brief of next season Y)

**11:00+ — Execution**
- Allocation manager pushes the moves into the WMS / DC.
- Buying assistants raise purchase orders for reorder list, contact suppliers.
- Merchandise planners update OTB log.
- Markdowns submitted to pricing system for activation at next price-update window.

**This 90-minute meeting is the unit of in-season management.** Aimily In-Season must produce outputs that drop into agenda step 3 (category review) and agenda step 6 (decisions log) verbatim. Per-SKU verdicts (current aimily v1) are the right output unit. The next surface to build (or the surface to refine) is a **trade-meeting-ready report** that gives each buyer their category's "agenda step 3" verbatim — best/worst, actions proposed, evidence + counter-evidence, confidence dims.

## What This Tells Us About aimily In-Season's Decision Verbs, Rationale Prose, and Mango Pitch Positioning

### Decision verbs — align verbatim with the trade-meeting decision set

Current aimily verbs (per the May 17 state doc): Kill · Markdown · Reponer N uds · Reducir compra · Investigar · Replicar estilo · Extender colores · Mantener.

**Verdict: 7/8 are perfect.** They map directly onto the practitioner-canonical decision set: Kill ≡ Cancel, Markdown ≡ Markdown, Reponer ≡ Reorder/Replenish, Reducir compra ≡ Cancel-portion / Hold, Investigar ≡ Investigate, Replicar estilo ≡ Replicate (cross-season learning), Extender colores ≡ Range extension / Push allocation, Mantener ≡ Hold/Maintain.

**Missing from the decision set vs the practitioner canonical**:
- **Acelerar intake / Pull forward** — when a winner needs more stock now, not next reorder cycle
- **Consolidar** — when two color or size buckets should merge
- **Push allocation** — re-allocate existing stock between channels/regions
- **CAYG markdown** (Clear As You Go) — a soft, early markdown distinct from "Markdown"

Of these, **"Pull forward intake"** and **"Push allocation"** are worth adding because they are top-of-mind for the trade meeting's decisions log. **"CAYG"** is implicit in graduated-markdown logic and probably doesn't need to be a separate verb.

### Rationale prose — speak Jackson-Shaw / Goworek vocabulary, not statistician's

Each SKU verdict in aimily should:
- Open with a 1-line **outcome statement** in trade-meeting verb language ("Reponer 240 unidades; agotamiento estimado en 11 días al ritmo actual")
- Follow with **evidence** stated in WSSI vocabulary (sell-through %, weeks of cover, ASP achieved, returns rate, margin effective)
- State **counter-evidence** (what could make this wrong)
- State **archetype context** (where this SKU sits in the lineage / family / archetype)
- State **confidence** across the 6 dims aimily already emits

This is the structure a buyer would prepare for trade-meeting agenda step 3. **Every SKU output should be trade-meeting-ready prose.**

### Mango pitch positioning — three framings to test

1. **"The institutional memory Mango doesn't have"** — Inditex has Go! and trains commercials internally. Mango doesn't. aimily In-Season is the system-of-record for forensic learnings that survives buyer turnover.

2. **"The forensic complement to your WGSN forecast"** — Mango buyers likely already subscribe to WGSN. Aimily doesn't compete; it inverts the time arrow. WGSN tells you what to back next season; aimily tells you what last season did and why. Integrate via tagged-SKU export.

3. **"The senior merch-director-on-demand"** — At Mango's scale (~3,000 stores, dozens of countries), the buyer-merchandiser pairs run weekly trade meetings without senior-director attention on every category every week. aimily attends every category's review with senior-director-grade analysis prebuilt.

The strongest of the three for a Mango cold pitch is **(3)** — it's the one most likely to feel like immediate ROI. (1) is the strategic moat argument for retention. (2) is the procurement-friendliness argument.
